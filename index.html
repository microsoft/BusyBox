BusyBox: Benchmarking Affordance Generalization

Modular, electronics-rich manipulation benchmark to test affordance generalization beyond simple pick-and-place.

Dean Fortier, Timothy Adamson, Tess Hellebrekers, Teresa LaScala, Kofi Ennin, Michael Murray, Andrey Kolobov, Galen Mullins
Paper (PDF) Code (Coming Soon) Dataset (coming soon)

7 task families · 3 configurations · 1,042 demonstrations

Overview

While Robot Foundation Models (RFMs) have shown impressive progress in generalizing across vision and language spaces, they still struggle with a critical capability: affordance generalization – the ability to manipulate new objects with familiar physical features. Basic interface elements like buttons and switches are designed to look and function similarly across different objects to facilitate this generalization for humans, yet current RFMs fail to achieve this basic level of understanding.

BusyBox addresses this gap by providing a systematic benchmark for evaluating how well RFMs can transfer their understanding of basic affordances (pressing buttons, flipping switches, turning knobs) to new configurations. Our experiments show that even state-of-the-art models like π0 completely fail at this seemingly simple task, highlighting a major area for improvement in robotic manipulation.

Contributions

  • BusyBox: A physical benchmark consisting of 6 swappable 3D-printed modules with switches, sliders, wires, buttons, a display, and a dial for systematic evaluation of RFMs' affordance generalization.
  • Experiment Protocol: A methodology for assessing whether models truly understand affordances or merely memorize spatial positions, using module reconfiguration to test zero-shot transfer.
  • Demonstration Dataset: 1,042 language-annotated trajectories (~4.8 hours) collected via Mobile ALOHA teleoperation, covering 7 task families with systematic state randomization.
  • Instrumented Evaluation: Electronics design enabling automated task completion detection via 10Hz state logging, supporting semi-automatic evaluation of multi-step tasks.
  • Baseline Results: Empirical evidence that even state-of-the-art models fail at basic affordance generalization (π0: 30% on trained configuration, 0% on novel arrangements).
  • Open-Source Release: CAD files for 3D printing, bill of materials for electronics, data collection protocols, and evaluation scripts to facilitate reproducible research.

BusyBox Hardware

BusyBox consists of six 3D-printable modules that can be easily swapped and rotated to create multiple configurations with the same set of affordances:

  • Display Module: E Ink display with LED indicators and main electronics
  • Buttons Module: Four colored, illuminated buttons
  • Sliders Module: Two adjustable sliders
  • Knob Module: Rotatable knob with 6 positions and handle
  • Switches Module: Two switches with on/off positions
  • Wire Module: Colored wires with pluggable connectors

The modular design enables rapid reconfiguration using snap connectors, allowing researchers to systematically vary the spatial layout while maintaining consistent affordances.

Benchmark Design

The BusyBox benchmark evaluates affordance generalization by training models on a canonical configuration and testing their ability to perform the same tasks on reconfigured BusyBox instances. This tests whether models truly understand the affordances or merely memorize spatial positions.

Task Families

  • Button Tasks: Press colored buttons (by color or position reference)
  • Slider Tasks: Move sliders to specific positions (1-5)
  • Knob Tasks: Rotate knob to positions (1-6) or directions
  • Switch Tasks: Flip switches on/off (requiring bimanual coordination)
  • Wire Pull Tasks: Pull out specific colored wires
  • Wire Insert Tasks: Insert wires into terminals
  • BusyBox Manipulation: Rotate or move the entire BusyBox

Results

Current Robot Foundation Models Fail at Affordance Generalization

Despite high success on conventional pick-and-place tasks, leading VLAs completely fail at BusyBox's affordance generalization challenge. Even after finetuning on our dataset, π0 achieves only 30% success on the training configuration and 0% on novel configurations.

Assembly

BusyBox's modular design enables rapid reconfiguration through snap connectors. Modules can be swapped and rotated in under 2 minutes, allowing researchers to quickly create new test configurations. The 3D-printed components are designed for easy replication in any robotics lab with access to a standard 3D printer. Two-filament printing enhances visibility of position markers, though single-color prints with manual highlighting work as an alternative.

Teleoperation Data Collection

We collected 1,042 demonstrations using Mobile ALOHA teleoperation on the canonical BusyBox configuration. The dataset covers all task families with systematic variation in:

  • Initial states (slider/knob positions, switch states)
  • BusyBox position and orientation
  • Language instruction (color vs position references)
  • Robot starting poses

Teleoperators followed strict protocols for consistency: efficient movements, active demonstration without unnecessary pauses, and ensuring wrist camera visibility of task-relevant areas.

Model Evaluation

The instrumented BusyBox automatically logs state changes at 10Hz, enabling precise success detection and progress tracking for multi-step tasks.

Our experiments revealed that the finetuned π0 model achieved limited success only on the training configuration (30%), failing completely on reconfigured layouts due to spatial memorization. Wire insertion proved impossible while simpler tasks like knob turning reached 50% success, highlighting significant gaps in affordance understanding.
